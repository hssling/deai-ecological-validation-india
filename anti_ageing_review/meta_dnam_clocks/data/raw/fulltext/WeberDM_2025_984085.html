Abstract

Objective:

Black Americans face disproportionate challenges related to substance use and the health impacts of substance use, including accelerated aging. Accordingly, interventions are needed that protect against the harmful effect of substance use on accelerated aging. The present study examined whether the ProSAAF intervention, a relationship education program designed to promote strong relationships among Black families, moderated the association between substance use and accelerated aging among Black couples.

Methods:

Black couples received either the ProSAAF intervention or a minimal-touch control intervention and provided DNA specimens for methylation-based biomarker measurements of alcohol use and cigarette smoking (ATS and cg05575921, combined into a substance use composite) and accelerated aging (DNAm GrimAge) 6- and 9-years post-intervention.

Results:

Participants receiving the ProSAAF intervention (relative to the control condition) experienced a significantly weaker association between substance use and accelerated aging. Among the participants who were in the same relationship as when they were initially randomized, the association between substance use and aging was significantly weaker in the ProSAAF condition than the control condition for participants with lower relationship satisfaction preintervention; no differences emerged among those with higher satisfaction.

Conclusions:

ProSAAF mitigated some of the harmful effect of substance use on accelerated aging among Black couples, particularly among those with lower pre-intervention relationship satisfaction. These findings highlight the potential for relationship interventions to serve as sources of constructed resilience that promote healthier aging.

Keywords: Black Americans, relationship intervention, substance use, accelerated aging, constructed resilience

Abstracto:

Objetivo:

Los Afroamericanos enfrentan desafíos desproporcionados relacionados con el uso de sustancias y los impactos del uso de sustancias en la salud, incluido el envejecimiento acelerado. En consecuencia, se necesitan intervenciones que protejan contra los efectos nocivos del consumo de sustancias sobre el envejecimiento acelerado. El presente estudio examinó si la intervención “Promoting Strong African American Families” (ProSAAF, por sus siglas en inglés), un programa de educación sobre relaciones diseñado para promover relaciones sólidas entre familias Negras, moderando la asociación entre el uso de sustancias y el envejecimiento acelerado entre las parejas Negras.

Métodos:

Las parejas Negras recibieron la intervención ProSAAF o una intervención de control de contacto mínimo y proporcionaron muestras de ADN para mediciones de biomarcadores basados ​​en metilación del consumo de alcohol y tabaquismo (ATS y cg05575921, combinados en un compuesto de uso de sustancias) y envejecimiento acelerado (DNAm GrimAge) 6 y 9 años después de la intervención.

Resultados:

Los participantes que recibieron la intervención ProSAAF (en relación con la condición de control) experimentaron una asociación significativamente más débil entre el uso de sustancias y el envejecimiento acelerado. Entre los participantes que tenían la misma relación que cuando fueron asignados al azar inicialmente, la asociación entre el uso de sustancias y el envejecimiento fue significativamente más débil en la condición ProSAAF que en la condición de control para los participantes con menor satisfacción en la relación antes de la intervención; no surgieron diferencias entre aquellos con mayor satisfacción.

Conclusiones:

ProSAAF mitigó algunos de los efectos nocivos del uso de sustancias sobre el envejecimiento acelerado entre las parejas Negras, particularmente entre aquellas con menor satisfacción en la relación antes de la intervención. Estos hallazgos resaltan el potencial de las intervenciones en las relaciones para servir como fuentes de resiliencia construida que promuevan un envejecimiento más saludable.

Black Americans have shorter lifespans than other racial groups in the United States (Arias et al., 2023), reflecting health disparities that include increased incidence of chronic health conditions (e.g., Daw, 2017) and accelerated aging (i.e., biological aging at a faster rate than chronological age). The weathering hypothesis argues that cumulative stress experiences stemming from systemic racism contribute to physiological responses that lead to accelerated aging (e.g., Geronimus et al., 2006; Geronimus, 2023). Empirical literature has robustly supported this hypothesis, demonstrating that myriad contextual stressors tied to systemic racism (e.g., racial discrimination, financial difficulties, neighborhood disorder) contribute to accelerated aging in Black Americans (e.g., Carter et al., 2019; Simons et al., 2019, 2021).

In an effort to further understand contributors to accelerated aging, research has increasingly highlighted a behavioral pathway that includes substance use (e.g., Beach et al., 2022; Kresovich et al., 2021; Lei et al., 2020). Alcohol and cigarette use contribute substantially to accelerated aging, with one study showing that biomarkers of cigarette smoking and alcohol consumption accounted for roughly half of the variance of two separate indicators of accelerated aging (Lei et al., 2022a). Although Black Americans on average consume tobacco and alcohol less than other racial groups (e.g., Center for Behavioral Health Statistics and Quality, 2015), they nevertheless experience greater negative consequences from tobacco (e.g., King et al., 2022) and alcohol consumption (e.g., Zapolski et al., 2014). Historical and ongoing legacies of racism contribute to substance use and its disproportionate impact among Black Americans. For instance, Black communities have received targeted marketing of specific products such as menthol cigarettes (King et al., 2022). Moreover, research has indicated greater substance use in response to experiences of discrimination as a result of depleted regulatory resources (e.g., Desalu et al., 2019). Accordingly, it is important to identify potential sources of resilience that may reduce some of the harmful effects of smoking and drinking on Black Americans’ health.

The Role of Relationship Enhancement

One important source of strength and resilience for Black Americans is a positive romantic relationship (e.g., Beach et al., 2019). In the context of accumulating stressors, having a partner who is a “safe base” may reduce physiological stress responses and inflammation (e.g., Beach et al., 2019; Slatcher & Selcuk, 2017) and potentially mitigate effects on accelerated aging. Accordingly, promoting strong relationships through interventions may be one way to “construct” resilience and thus buffer the impact of substance use on accelerated aging. Although this specific hypothesis has not been tested, there is a large body of research showing that couple-based interventions benefit relationship quality (e.g., Roddy et al., 2020; Stanley et al., 2020) and individual health (e.g., Fischer et al., 2016).

One relationship intervention with particular promise for promoting strong relationships among Black Americans is the Protecting Strong African American Families (ProSAAF) intervention. ProSAAF is a family-centered prevention program designed to promote the couple, coparenting, and parent-child relationships of Black couples coparenting a preadolescent child in the rural South (see Barton et al., 2018). The ProSAAF intervention aimed to help Black couples in the rural South jointly navigate commonly experienced external stressors (e.g., racial discrimination, financial strain), creating “constructed resilience” to the impact of an oppressive context (see Beach et al., 2023). Numerous studies have highlighted the benefits of ProSAAF for relationship functioning (e.g., Barton et al., 2018a; Barton et al., 2018b; Weber et al., 2024) and individual functioning (e.g., Barton et al., 2018b; Barton et al., 2021; Beach et al., 2023). Further, some recent evidence has demonstrated that ProSAAF may help buffer the impact of contextual stressors (i.e., neighborhood disadvantage, financial strain) on biomarkers of accelerated aging (Lei & Beach, 2022; Lei et al., 2022b).

The Present Study

The present study aimed to build on this work by testing whether the ProSAAF intervention would buffer the impact of cigarette and alcohol use—substances strongly associated with accelerated aging, chronic health conditions, and premature death (e.g., Lei et al., 2022a)—on accelerated aging among Black Americans. Participants received either the ProSAAF intervention or a low/minimal touch control intervention and participated in follow-ups approximately 6- and 9-years later assessing specific biomarkers of substance use and accelerated aging. Given that self-reports of alcohol consumption and smoking behaviors can substantially underestimate use (e.g., Lei et al., 2022a), we used DNA methylation-based biomarkers of alcohol and cigarette smoking to provide objective and highly sensitive indicators of substance use (Miller et al., 2020; Philibert et al., 2019). Although a variety of indicators of accelerated aging exist in the literature, we focused on the composite biomarker indicator DNAm GrimAge given its robust associations with chronic health conditions, health behaviors such as substance use, and time-to-death (Lu et al., 2019).

The primary aim of this study was to examine if receiving the ProSAAF intervention (relative to the control intervention) moderated the association between DNA methylation-based biomarkers of substance use and GrimAge at 6- and 9-years post-intervention. Given evidence from this sample that the ProSAAF intervention has multiple benefits for couples’ relationships (e.g., Barton et al., 2018a) and buffers the impact of contextual stressors on accelerated aging at the 6-year follow up (Lei & Beach, 2022; Lei et al., 2022b), we hypothesized that participation in ProSAAF relative to the control condition might attenuate the impact of substance use on aging. Importantly, these prior studies examining ProSAAF’s effects on aging in our sample used a different indicator of accelerated aging. GrimAge has been shown to be associated with mortality independent of family relatedness, chronological age, physical activity, body mass index, smoking, education, or chronic diseases, and to be more predictive of mortality than other indicators of accelerated aging (Fohr et al., 2023). Thus, examining GrimAge extends prior results and tests how the buffering effects of ProSAAF generalize to other health and aging outcomes.

As a secondary aim, we examined whether relationship satisfaction at baseline affected the degree to which these buffering effects occurred. This aim was informed by research indicating that intervention effects can differ based on pre-existing characteristics at the start of an intervention, with some evidence suggesting that partners with more relationship challenges at baseline benefit most from relationship education programs (Stanley et al., 2020). Our recent research similarly suggested that ProSAAF’s benefits on long-term relationship satisfaction were most pronounced for partners who began the intervention with relatively lower satisfaction (Weber et al., 2024). Accordingly, we wondered whether those who began the study with relatively lower satisfaction would be more likely to demonstrate greater buffering effects of ProSAAF participation relative to the control group on the association between substance use and accelerated aging. We considered this hypothesis exploratory, however, given that this is the first time we tested potential consequences of differential treatment response for buffering.

Method

We report how we determined our sample size, all data exclusions, all manipulations, and all measures in the study. All procedures were approved by the Institutional Review Board at the University of Georgia. The analyses were not preregistered. Materials and analysis code are available by request from the corresponding author.

Participants

Couples with a Black preadolescent or early-adolescent child living in the home were recruited by mail and phone via advertisements in their communities and through lists from local schools (see Barton et al., 2018). Eligible couples had to be living together, in a relationship for ≥ 2 years, and coparenting a Black child in the targeted age range for ≥ 1 year.

The baseline sample of 346 families were randomized to intervention (n = 172 couples) or control (n = 174 couples). Of the total sample, 63% were married. Married couples had been married for an average of 9.8 years (SD = 7.48) and unmarried couples had been living together for an average of 6.7 years (SD = 5.42). Average age for men was 39.9 years old (SD = 9.6) and for women was 36.6 years old (SD = 7.45). The majority (51%) had incomes <100% of the federal poverty level, and another 17% had incomes between 100–150% of that level. Median monthly income for men was $1,375 (SD = $1,375) and for women was $1,220 (SD = $1,440). Median education for men was high school or GED and for women was some college or trade school. Most were mixed-gender couples (n = 344).

Given our focus on accelerated aging, our analyzed sample consists of individuals who provided biomarker indicators of aging at Wave 5 (six years after baseline) and/or Wave 6 (nine years after baseline). Because of missing data from one or both partners at one or both waves, this resulted in a final sample of 661 total observations across 383 individual partners comprising 221 couples. Partners included in the final analyzed sample did not differ significantly with regard to baseline characteristics (including reported demographic characteristics and baseline relationship satisfaction) from those missing aging data at W5 and W6. The majority (67.3%) of participants in this analyzed sample reported at W5 and/or W6 that they were still in the relationship they were in at the start of the ProSAAF trial, comprising 445 total observations across 279 individuals within 153 couples.1

Procedures

Project staff visited families’ homes and obtained informed consent, after which family members separately completed Wave 1 (W1) measures. Families were randomized to intervention or control. Families were visited for Wave 2 (W2), Wave 3 (W3), and Wave 4 (W4) assessments a mean of 9.4 months, 17.0 months, and 24.5 months respectively after W1. In 2019 (6 years after W1), participants were recontacted about follow-up assessments focused on healthy aging.2 W5 was then completed in early 20203 and W6 was completed in 2023.

The ProSAAF program was delivered in couples’ homes by faciliators drawn from local communities who had received training in the program content. The intervention was provided over six weeks between W1 and W2. Each of the two-hour sessions focused on a variety of stressors common for rural Black families (e.g., racism, finances; Bryant et al., 2010). Cognitive and behavioral techniques were used to help the couple respond to stressors in effective ways, and enhance coparenting. Video presentations taught program content and the facilitator guided the couple through the video instruction, structured activities, and discussions. The first 60 min of each session focused on the couple relationship, and the next 30 min focused on parenting/coparenting topics. The facilitator met with the target youth for a 15-min individual activity. Finally, the entire family met with the facilitator for a 15-min joint activity. Booster sessions to reinforce content were scheduled 3 and 9 months after program completion (about 2 months before W2 and about 4 months before W3). Of the families assigned to the intervention, 81% completed all 6 sessions, 73% completed booster session 1, and 59% completed booster session 2 (see Barton et al., 2018 for more details).

Couples in the control condition were assessed on the same schedule, controlling for effects of repeated measurements, maturation, individual differences, and external social changes. After the pretest, control couples were mailed the book 12 hr to a Great Marriage (Markman et al., 2004) and a workbook that included reasons for enhancing the couple’s relationship, guidelines, communication and problem-solving strategies, and exercises designed to improve relationships. Couples’ use of the workbook was not assessed.

At W5 and W6 participants provided self-report data via phone interview or online survey. For those who consented to provide a blood sample at W5 and/or W6, a certified phlebotomist visited the home and collected 6 tubes of blood (30 ml) from each participant. Blood samples were delivered to the Psychiatric Genetics Lab at the University of Iowa. DNA was prepared via standard procedures and stored at −20°C until used. Epigenome wide methylation assessments were conducted using Illumina InfiniumMethylationEpic Beadchip array (a.k.a. EPIC) by the University of Minnesota Genome Center according to the manufacturer’s (Illumina, San Diego) instructions. Samples were randomized with respect to slide and position on arrays to minimize batch effects as recommended by the Illumina Infinium Protocol Guide. The resulting data were DASEN normalized using the MethyLumi (Davis & Bilke, 2010), WateRmelon (Wong et al., 2013) and IlluminaHumanMethylationEPICanno. ilm10b2.hg19 (Illumina) R packages as per our prior procedures (Dogan et al., 2015). CpG values were background-corrected using the “noob” method. Finally, data were subjected to standard sample and probe level quality control measures.

Measures

Epigenetic aging (i.e., DNA-methylation based aging) was assessed at W5 and W6 using the array data described above. DNAm GrimAge (see Lu et al., 2019) is an indicator of epigenetic aging optimized to capture mortality risk, comprising multiple DNAm biomarkers of physiological risk factors. GrimAge has been robustly associated with time-to-death, time-to-coronary heart disease, time-to-cancer, and age-at-menopause (e.g., Lu et al., 2019). We estimated GrimAge using the publicly available tool hosted by the Horvath Lab at the University of California, Los Angeles (https://dnamage.genetics.ucla.edu/). GrimAge is scored in estimated year units, such that a higher value corresponds to a higher estimated epigenetic age. When chronological age is controlled, associations with GrimAge can be interpreted as associations with accelerated aging (i.e., deviation from expected GrimAge given chronological age).

Substance use was assessed at W5 and W6 using a composite of biomarkers assessing cigarette and alcohol use. Methylation sensitive digital PCR (MSdPCR) was used to assess level of cg05575921 methylation (Dcg05575921) and was used as the indicator of cigarette use4 (hereafter abbreviated “cg055”). Alcohol T scores (ATS) values were an unweighted sum of four z-scores of MSdPCR assays at four loci (cg02583484, cg04987734, cg09935388 and cg04583842) previously shown to capture variation in heavy alcohol use (Miller et al., 2020), and were used as the indicator of alcohol use. Cg055 and ATS scores were generated using the same samples of DNA. Primer probe sets were provided by Behavioral Diagnostics (Coralville, IA) and both droplet digital PCR reagents and equipment were obtained from Bio-Rad (Hercules, CA) and used according to previously described protocols (e.g., Philibert et al., 2019). Raw values of cg055 were expressed as “% methylation” and interpreted such that lower values correspond to higher chronic cigarette use, whereas higher ATS values are indicative of chronic heavy alcohol consumption. ATS and cg055 values were moderately correlated (r = −.55). The z-scores of ATS and cg055 (reversed so that ATS and cg055 scores could be interpreted in the same direction) were then averaged to create a composite score of overall substance use, with a higher score corresponding to more combined alcohol and cigarette use.

Relationship satisfaction was assessed at W1 using the Quality of Marriage Index (QMI; Norton, 1983). Participants rated agreement on a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly disagree to 5 = Strongly agree) for the first five items (e.g., “My partner and I have a good relationship”). Participants answered the final item (“Which best describes the degree of happiness, everything considered, in your relationship?”) on a five-point Likert scale (1 = Very unhappy to 5 = Perfectly happy). The six items were summed to create total scores, with higher scores indicating greater relationship satisfaction (αwomen = .93; αmen = .89).

Results

Analyses were conducted in Stata (StataCorp, 2017) using the mixed procedure for analyzing multilevel data (i.e., when individual observations are not independent). In these data, time (W5 and W6) was nested within individual partners who were nested within couples, thus constituting three-level data.

Analyses were conducted on the total sample with GrimAge data at W5 and/or W6. Given missing data for specific predictors,5 645 observations from 378 partners within 218 couples were included in total in the final model. In addition, given that initial relationship satisfaction and intervention were focal to the initial relationship, we also conducted sensitivity analyses in which the same models were conducted using only the subsample of partners who reported that they were in the same relationship as when they entered the study; after accounting for missing data, this resulted in 435 data points from 274 individual partners in 150 couples.

Preliminary Analyses

Descriptive statistics for and correlations among study variables are presented in Supplemental Table S1. Of note, intervention condition was not significantly associated at the bivariate level with substance use or GrimAge, indicating that ProSAAF participation did not have a direct effect on the overall level of substance use or overall epigenetic aging.

To establish the association between substance use and GrimAge after accounting for potential covariates, we ran a three-level multilevel model (MLM) in which GrimAge was regressed onto participants’ chronological age, sex, poverty status, and substance use, as represented by this series of equations:

where t indexes time (W5 and W6), i indexes individuals, and j indexes couples; u00j represents a random intercept at the level of the couple, r0ij represents a random intercept at the level of the individual, and etij is the error term. Given that chronological age is highly associated with GrimAge (Lu et al., 2019), chronological age was included so that effects of interest could be interpreted as predicting accelerated aging, over and above chronological age. Chronological age was grand-mean centered for analysis. Sex (coded as −0.5 = female, 0.5 = male) and poverty (coded as −0.5 = above 150% of poverty line, 0.5 = below 150% of poverty line) were included as covariates given their association with GrimAge (Lu et al., 2019).

Full results of the preliminary analyses are available in Supplemental Table S2. As expected, substance use was significantly positively associated with GrimAge, such that higher substance use predicted higher GrimAge, over and above other significant and expected associations between chronological age, sex, and poverty status with GrimAge.6 These findings were consistent in the total sample and the subsample of individuals in the initial relationship.

Aim 1: The Moderating Effect of Intervention on Substance Use and GrimAge

To examine whether participation in ProSAAF moderated the effect of substance use on GrimAge, we ran a three-level MLM in which GrimAge was regressed onto the set of predictors included in the preliminary model (i.e., chronological age, sex, poverty status, and substance use7) as well as intervention status (ProSAAF or control condition) as a Level-3 (couple level) predictor and the two-way, cross-level interaction between intervention and substance use. Intervention condition was coded such that −0.5 = Control group and 0.5 = ProSAAF.

Results of this model are reported in Table 1. These analyses indicated a significant two-way interaction between substance use and intervention condition. As demonstrated in Figure 1, partners in both conditions demonstrated a positive association between substance use and GrimAge, such that higher levels of substance use predicted higher GrimAge. However, this association was weaker among individuals who received the ProSAAF intervention (B = 3.08, SE = 0.16, t = 19.12, p < .001) than among individuals in the control condition (B = 3.53, SE = 0.14, t = 25.58, p < .001), indicating that ProSAAF reduced (i.e., buffered) the association of substance use with accelerated aging.

Aim 2: The Moderating Effect of Initial Relationship Satisfaction and Intervention on Substance Use and GrimAge

To examine the effect of initial satisfaction alongside intervention and substance use on GrimAge, we ran a three-level MLM in which GrimAge was regressed onto the set of predictors in the Aim 1 model (i.e., chronological age, sex, poverty status, substance use, intervention, and the two-way interaction between intervention and substance use) along with relationship satisfaction (at W1, prior to the intervention) as a Level 2 (individual level) predictor, the two-way interaction between intervention and relationship satisfaction, the two-way interaction between substance use and relationship satisfaction, and the three-way interaction between intervention, substance use, and relationship satisfaction. Relationship satisfaction was grand-mean centered for analysis.

Results of this model are reported in Table 2. These analyses showed a significant two-way interaction between substance use and treatment condition. Consistent with the results from Aim 1, the association between substance use and accelerated aging was weaker among individuals who received the ProSAAF intervention (B = 3.04, SE = 0.17, t = 17.39, p < .001) than among individuals in the control condition (B = 3.53, SE = 0.14, t = 25.42, p < .001). Neither of the two-way interactions including relationship satisfaction were significant (ps > .607). The three-way interaction between intervention, substance use, and relationship satisfaction was also not significant (p = .183).

Sensitivity Analysis Among Individuals Still in the Initial Relationship

Within the subsample of individuals still in the same relationship as when they received the intervention (see Table 2), the two-way interaction between substance use and intervention remained statistically significant (p = .050) and was consistent in magnitude and direction with the effect observed for the total sample. Additionally, a significant three-way interaction emerged between intervention, substance use, and relationship satisfaction, indicating that the buffering effect of ProSAAF varied as a function of initial satisfaction. We probed this interaction using the pick-a-point approach, plotting associations between substance use and GrimAge within each condition at 1 SD above and below the mean of relationship satisfaction (referred to as “higher” and “lower” satisfaction, respectively). We then conducted the Dawson and Richter (2006) test for differences between slopes to test if the simple slopes differed by condition at higher (+1 SD) and lower (−1 SD) levels of relationship satisfaction.

Simple slopes are depicted in Figure 2. Among partners who were initially lower in satisfaction (Figure 2A), those in the control condition had a significantly stronger positive association between substance use and GrimAge (B = 3.79, SE = 0.30, t = 12.53, p < .001) relative to those who received the ProSAAF intervention (B = 2.52, SE = 0.37, t = 6.85, p < .001; slope difference test: t = −2.69, p = .008). In contrast, partners with initially higher relationship satisfaction (Figure 2B) did not significantly differ in their simple slopes across conditions (higher satisfaction, control condition: B = 3.31, SE = 0.23, t = 14.17, p < .001; higher satisfaction, ProSAAF condition: B = 3.49, SE = 0.25, t = 13.70, p < .001; slope difference test: t = 0.52, p = .606). Thus, among the subsample of participants in the same relationship at the time they were randomized to the ProSAAF intervention or control condition, buffering effects of the intervention were specific to partners with lower initial relationship satisfaction.

Supplemental Analyses

Given that prior studies from our sample found that the ProSAAF intervention buffered the impact of contextual stressors on a different indicator of accelerated aging (DunedinPoAm), we also ran the models specified above with DunedinPoAm as a covariate. Including DunedinPoAm did not change the pattern of results (see Supplemental Table S5).

Discussion

This study examined whether the ProSAAF intervention, a relationship education program designed to promote strong relationships and reduce the impact of contextual stressors among Black families, buffered the association for another driver of accelerated aging: substance use. Employing innovative, DNA-methylation based indicators (a composite of biomarkers of cigarette and alcohol use and GrimAge to assess epigenetic aging), our preliminary analyses supported evidence indicating a robust association between higher levels of substance use and higher epigenetic age (e.g., Lei et al., 2022a). The primary results revealed that ProSAAF may reduce these harmful effects, offering novel insights about the broad impact of the ProSAAF intervention and how relationships can shape health and aging.

Our primary set of findings demonstrated that for the total sample, participants receiving the ProSAAF intervention (relative to those receiving a minimal-touch control intervention in which they received a relationship workbook) experienced a significantly weaker association between substance use and accelerated aging. In other words, the ProSAAF intervention had a buffering effect on the harmful impact of substance use on accelerated aging. Notably, intervention condition was not directly associated with substance use in our preliminary analyses, indicating that ProSAAF did not directly affect levels of substance use; thus, the specific effect of ProSAAF was to alter the degree to which substance use was associated with accelerated aging. Importantly, the same pattern of results emerged among the subsample of partners who reported being in the same relationship as when they received the intervention (albeit the finding reduced below the threshold of statistical significance) and within the model that also accounted for preintervention relationship satisfaction. These findings are striking given that the intervention occurred approximately 6–9 years prior to measurements of substance use and accelerated aging, highlighting long-lasting impacts of the intervention.

These findings build on the body of evidence with this sample showing the long-term benefits of the ProSAAF intervention for relationship functioning (e.g., Barton et al., 2018) and individual functioning (e.g., Barton et al., 2021), along with the potential for ProSAAF to buffer the negative impact of contextual stressors on a different indicator of epigenetic aging (Lei & Beach, 2022; Lei et al., 2022b). These previous findings are consistent with ProSAAF’s aim of promoting positive couple, parenting, and family interactions and preventing stress spillover from stressors commonly experienced among Black families (see Barton et al., 2018). Notably, however, substance use was not a target of ProSAAF. As such, the present findings suggest that the ProSAAF intervention may have benefits extending beyond the focus of the program itself.

These effects raise important questions regarding the mechanisms through which the intervention may buffer the impact of substance use on accelerated aging. For instance, it is possible that ProSAAF altered the social context in which smoking and alcohol use occurred. Research has indicated that alcohol use and smoking among Black Americans can be linked to physiological stress responses (e.g., Cavanagh & Obasi, 2022) and used as methods of coping with external stressors such as racial discrimination (e.g., Desalu et al., 2019). Given that ProSAAF focused on how couples contend together with external stressors, it is possible that ProSAAF helped couples use more relational approaches to coping. As a result, substance use may have become less strongly tied to stress responses and deleterious effects on aging relative to those in the control condition. This possibility would be consistent with research suggesting that having a partner who is a “safe base” during times of stress may reduce physiological stress responses and promote better health behaviors (e.g., Slatcher & Selcuk, 2017), which could confer benefits for many outcomes including their epigenetic aging. Further research will be important to examine the mechanisms that explain these effects, including changes in relationships over time (e.g., increases in relational coping and relationship quality).

Our second set of findings provided some evidence that preintervention relationship satisfaction may impact the magnitude of the ProSAAF buffering effect, and so adds to the discussion of who benefits from relationship interventions. Among the subset of participants who were in the same relationship as when they were randomized to condition, the positive association between substance use and aging was significantly weaker in the ProSAAF condition than the control condition for participants who began the study with lower satisfaction. In contrast, among those with higher satisfaction, the association between substance use and aging was comparable for those in the ProSAAF and control conditions. In essence, this finding illustrates that the buffering effect of ProSAAF on the harmful impact of substance use on aging was most pronounced among those entering the intervention with lower satisfaction. This pattern was consistent with our hypotheses based on research suggesting that couples with more relationship challenges may receive the most benefit from relationship education programs in general (see Stanley et al., 2020) and in this sample specifically (Weber et al., 2024), possibly because these couples have the most room to grow from intervention. Importantly, this past research has focused on how interventions benefit relationship health. Thus, the present findings are striking in demonstrating that relationship characteristics present at intervention onset may have a bearing on the relative effects of an intervention on individual health several years later.

In interpreting the present results, limitations of the study should also be considered. The present study focused on DNA-methylation indicators of substance use (an aggregate of separate biomarkers of cigarette smoking and alcohol consumption) and epigenetic aging (DNAm GrimAge) at 6- and/or 9-year follow-up. Although the longitudinal nature of this study is an important strength, extended follow-ups necessarily result in attrition. Our analyzed sample did not differ from the sample collected at baseline in demographic characteristics or baseline relationship satisfaction; however, it is possible that those who provided blood samples differed in other ways. Additionally, we selected these specific indicators of substance use and epigenetic aging based on their strong validity (e.g., Lu et al., 2019; Philibert et al., 2019); however, given the variety of different indicators of aging and substance use which emphasize different component parts, we cannot assume that these findings would generalize across all possible measures. For instance, given that our indicators of substance use capture chronic use, other substance use indicators capturing different time frames might have different effects. Additionally, results may differ across indicators of aging. Importantly, we controlled for the aging indicator from prior research using this sample (DunedinPoAm; Lei & Beach, 2022; Lei et al., 2022b) and found that the results remained consistent. Nonetheless, further research is needed examining different outcomes in other samples. Moreover, further research is needed to test mechanisms for this intervention effect, such as post-intervention changes to relationships that could alter the context in which substance use affects aging. In addition, the sample consisted of primarily mixed-gender Black couples enrolled in the ProSAAF trial living in the rural Southeastern U.S. who were primarily low-income and coparenting an adolescent child. As such, the findings may or may not generalize to different interventions; different regions; same-gender, trans, or nonbinary couples; couples with higher income; or couples without children, to name a few dimensions of interest for future research. Finally, although there was a wide range of relationship satisfaction scores, the sample was on average satisfied; thus, findings may not generalize to couples with clinical levels of relationship distress.

Notwithstanding these limitations, the present findings have implications for understanding the effects of the ProSAAF intervention and for the field of relationship interventions generally. Findings indicated that ProSAAF buffered the harmful impact of substance use on accelerated aging relative to those receiving a minimal-touch control intervention, with some evidence that this buffering effect may have been especially pronounced among those beginning the intervention with lower satisfaction. Given that ProSAAF was designed to generally promote positive relationships and foster “constructed resilience” to external stressors experienced by Black families and did not target substance use, these findings suggest that ProSAAF may have offered benefits for couples that indirectly affected the way that their substance use related to accelerated aging. Although future research is needed to explicate the mechanisms underlying these findings, these results extend the growing body of evidence on the efficacy of ProSAAF and highlight that this intervention can have benefits beyond specific treatment targets. These findings build on the robust literature on the benefits of relationship interventions for individual health and well-being (e.g., Fischer et al., 2016), including substance use. Further investigation of the health benefits of ProSAAF and other couple interventions can inform clinical practice such that couples receive targeted and effective interventions to promote positive relationships, health behaviors, and healthy aging long-term. In conjunction with large-scale systemic interventions that combat systems of oppression that contribute to disproportionately harmful health effects of substance use on Black Americans, ProSAAF and other couple interventions may play an important role in promoting better health and well-being.

Public Health Significance Statement:

Due to the effects of systemic racism, Black Americans experience disproportionately negative consequences from substance use, including increased accelerated aging (biological aging at a faster rate than chronological age). This study demonstrates that the ProSAAF intervention, a relationship education program designed to promote family resilience to the harsh environments experienced by Black families in rural Southern communities, reduces some of these harmful effects among Black couples. These beneficial effects of ProSAAF may be more pronounced for couples with lower initial relationship satisfaction.
