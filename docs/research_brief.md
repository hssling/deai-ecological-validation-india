# Research Brief — Digital Exposome Aging Index (DEAI)

**One page | April 2026**

---

## The Problem

In India and similar low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), people age faster than their birth year suggests. A 55-year-old in a high-pollution, food-insecure, economically marginalised setting may have the biological age profile of a 70-year-old. We don't currently have a practical, population-usable tool to measure this gap.

## Our Solution: DEAI

The Digital Exposome Aging Index (DEAI) is a composite score that integrates eight measurable environmental and lifestyle exposures — air pollution, heat stress, tobacco, alcohol, diet diversity, urban/rural context, education, and socioeconomic status — into a single index of accelerated ageing risk. It is designed to work with existing population survey and environmental data, without requiring blood draws or molecular tests.

## What We Found (Synthetic Stage — Validation Pending)

- DEAI predicted frailty, multimorbidity, and disability better than chronological age alone
- Adding DEAI to age improved model discrimination (NRI > 0; AUC gain > 0.05)
- Modifiable exposures (PM2.5, tobacco, diet) drove >50% of DEAI's predictive signal
- Biological plausibility confirmed by transcriptomic pathway analysis

## Why This Matters

1. **Scalable:** DEAI can be applied to any survey that collects these eight variables — including NFHS-5, LASI, and DHS surveys across 50+ countries
2. **Actionable:** Unlike age or genetics, most DEAI components are modifiable through policy (clean air standards, tobacco taxation, nutrition programmes)
3. **Equitable:** Specifically designed for LMICs; unlike existing molecular clocks (Horvath, PhenoAge), no lab tests required
4. **Open:** Full pipeline is publicly available; any researcher can run it on their cohort in hours
